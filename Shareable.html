Overview

Shareable is an app prototype that my team and I created for "Technology and Human Interaction," a class taught by Darren Gergle at Northwestern University. Our project was to find a problem for college students and design a tablet app to solve that problem. Our concept is an app that allows college students to easily lend and borrow clothes from their friends. We created low and high-fidelity prototypes and conducted one round of user testing for our app.

User Interviews

We were interested in how college students use platforms like Facebook Marketplace to buy and sell clothes, but our user interviews led us in a different direction. One student told us about how their dance group often requires themed outfits for different performances. Rather than buy new items for every show, the members of the group use instant messaging to offer up clothes to lend to others in the group.

Similarly, another student we interviewed described how her sorority holds an informal "Dress Bank" before every formal and before recruitment events. The Dress Bank is a clothing rack located in the sorority house basement where members leave old dresses for their sisters to borrow. Often, girls who lend their dresses have no idea who takes them off the rack to borrow. Most often, it takes a week or two to get the dresses back. On rare occasions, the dresses go missing, and there is no record of a borrower to be held accountable.

Concept

We recognized a need for a platform to lend and borrow clothes among peers. Other services like Facebook Marketplace, thredUP, and Poshmark allow individual sellers to list their clothes for (generally) cheap prices, but do not allow users to borrow and lend.

Shareable allows users to create and join groups of peers that they want to share clothes with. Users upload their own clothes to their virtual closet, where they can describe the item, tag it with relevant filters, and mark its availability to be borrowed. The user can see all of the items of clothing uploaded by their friends and filter the search to find exactly what they're looking for. Lenders and borrowers can direct message within the app to hash out the details of the exchange.

Low-fidelity Prototype

Below, you can see some of the pages from our first paper prototype. We mimicked user interaction using post-it notes, paper frames, and good old-fashioned pen and pencil. You can see the red pen from edits we made to the prototype after our first round of user testing.

To recruit participants, we reached out to students who matched up with our different user profiles. We had them interact with the prototype to test the flow of the app, timed how long it took participants to complete simple tasks, interviewed participants about their thoughts, and asked them to complete a short survey with feedback. After our second round of testing, we compiled all our feedback and started to create our final prototype.

Shareable Branding and UI Design

There is very little I enjoy more than drawing in Illustrator, so I was thrilled to take on Shareable's branding and overall visual design. As a group, we decided on a general color scheme (primarily purple for our alma mater), and I started iterating logos. I created the globe and shirt icons using Illustrator and chose the title font. Then I worked with my teammate Lauren to flesh out the design and select fonts and colors for headers, subheaders, text, and different buttons.

High-fidelity Prototype

I created this high-fidelity prototype of Shareable in Figma with two of my teammates, Alyssa Zhong and Julia Brain.

As a group we collaborated to create assets, tweak screen layouts, and edit each others' work. I was primarily responsible for the Direct Messaging home and conversation screens, the form to upload an item of clothing to lend, and half of the account information screens.
