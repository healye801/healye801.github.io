Luxury Rental Business Dashboard

As founding designer for a boat rental startup, I designed an owner dashboard for managing boat rentals, built a reusable component library to enable rapid product development, and created designs for booking management tools and consumer-facing pages—setting the design direction as the company scales.

Role:

Sole product designer

Team:

1 designer, 2 developers, 1 product manager

Tools:

Figma

Skills:

Wireframing, design system management, high-fidelity prototyping

Timeline:

August 2025 - Present

Opportunity

Luxury boat rental agencies in vacation destinations like Miami and Punta Cana face a critical digital problem. These small businesses rely on word-of-mouth, Google reviews, and search rankings to attract customers, but their booking processes consistently draw complaints. Most owners cobble together solutions using separate third-party vendors—one for listings, another for bookings—creating fragmented experiences that frustrate both business owners and vacationers.

Wavebooker identified an opportunity to streamline operations for these small luxury operators by combining listing management and booking into a single platform, reducing costs and complexity. The owner dashboard I designed is the first of two core products. The vision: create a seamless ecosystem where boat owners manage their business efficiently, while customers can search, filter, and book across multiple vendors through a centralized experience.

As founding designer, I was brought on to transform this concept into reality. The challenge was unique: luxury boat rentals serve people on vacation who expect to feel pampered and cared for. The brand's visual identity needed to communicate luxury and sophistication while delivering a simple, straightforward experience for busy business owners who just want their operations to run smoothly.

Challenge

Small luxury boat rental operators were drowning in operational overhead. Most relied on homegrown WordPress sites that were difficult to maintain and lacked the polish their clientele expected. These business owners are too busy running their operations to worry about website design, SEO, or user experience—but they knew their digital presence was costing them customers.

The pain points were clear:

01.

Fragmented touchpoints: Customers had to call to get basic information about their rental, creating friction in the booking process

02.

Unprofessional appearance: DIY websites didn't match the luxury experience owners wanted to provide

03.

Disconnected systems: Separate tools for listings, bookings, and communication meant nothing talked to each other

04.

Lost revenue: Owners suspected they were losing bookings to competitors with more polished digital experiences

From a design perspective, I faced several constraints. I was working with just three developers, which meant I needed to design efficiently and prioritize ruthlessly. Access to boat owners was sporadic, requiring me to validate decisions strategically rather than through continuous user feedback. The startup needed to ship quickly to prove the concept and secure funding, compressing timelines significantly.

I also had to design for a diverse user base—from owners with a single boat to operators managing fleets of 10+ vessels with multiple employees needing dashboard access. Most importantly, every component I created needed to work not just for the current owner dashboard, but for the upcoming customer-facing booking platform as well.

The biggest challenge? Creating an interface that felt luxurious and trustworthy to high-end clientele while remaining dead-simple for time-strapped business owners to manage.

Process

I designed a responsive web application optimized for both mobile and desktop using the Tailwind framework. The founder had conducted foundational user research through interviews with boat rental owners in our target markets, giving me a starting point to understand core business needs. From there, I took ownership of the design direction.

I began with competitive research and analysis to understand how to differentiate Wavebooker's brand and communicate both luxury and operational ease. I studied existing booking platforms, luxury service applications, and dashboard interfaces to identify opportunities for differentiation. This research informed not just visual design decisions, but also helped me leverage dashboard design best practices to create components that would scale across both current and future products.

My design process followed a strategic, iterative approach:

I started with a competitive analysis presentation to align the team on design direction and market positioning. Then I moved quickly into very low-fidelity wireframes, designing mobile-first while simultaneously considering desktop layouts.

A critical early step was creating future-looking wireframes to present to the founder.

These conceptual designs helped us think beyond the MVP and determine what components and patterns would need to scale as the product evolved. This forward-thinking approach ensured we weren't designing ourselves into a corner.

With that long-term vision established, we scoped back to the absolute essentials and used the wireframes to finalize requirements. This was crucial—by aligning on scope through low-fidelity designs, we avoided costly changes later in the process.

Once requirements were locked, I translated the low-fidelity wireframes into high-fidelity pages in Figma, establishing visual design standards and component patterns. Throughout the design process, I tested against Section 508 accessibility standards to ensure compliance from the start rather than retrofitting later.

Solution

We took our research findings and developed a comprehensive redesign that addressed each major painpoint:

  • Overhauled the tagging system to prominently highlight AI-generated tags, improving transparency and user trust
  • Introduced new filters allowing users to narrow down results and find relevant content faster
  • Streamlined commenting and tagging workflows to match design standards and increase efficiency
  • Improved navigation flows to reduce friction and enhance overall platform usability
Owner Dashboard

This was one of the first and most visible applications of AI in the client’s ecosystem, making it critical to set clear boundaries between AI-generated content and user-generated input and establish a design system for AI tools. Initial user feedback on AI was mixed-to-positive, with some users feeling hesitant about the integration. We want to set a precedent of transparency, user control, and trust.

We introduced the magic wand icon, an optional AI purple colorway, and an expanding badge component. These visual cues help users identify AI interactions easily and set a scalable standard forupcoming AI tools.

AI tagging could be a big help to these users, but they have to trust it. I’m hopeful that thoughtful design can convince even the most stalwart skeptic to give it a try.

Design System

Our research showed that users often used comments as makeshift tags because scrolling through a long tag list was just too tedious. This workaround was fast but led to files cluttered with pseudo-tag comments, making genuine annotations hard to spot. To fix this, we switched to a typeahead dropdown, making it quicker and easier to find the right tag.

We separated user-generated tags from AI-generated ones, with any edits to AI tags automatically moving them to the user-generated section. To keep improving AI accuracy, we worked with the machine learning team to capture data on any tagging inaccuracies.

Key Takeaways

Leading this project was a major responsibility and fantastic learning experience. Speaking with real users to learn about their day-to-day frustrations and motivations was enlightening. I made impactful design decisions ranging from UI updates, to new feature designs, to tagging taxonomy. I presented work to the client in multiple contexts including brainstorming sessions, usability study recaps, and legal reviews. I learned a lot about advocating for design in consulting contexts and how to relate human-centered design principles to business priorities.

And at the end of the day, I am confident that the new product will address real usability issues and scale to accommodate changing business interests.
