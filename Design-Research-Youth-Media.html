Design Research in Youth Media

20 weeks of research and design for the Northwestern Knight Lab Studio.
 Team: Prof. Zach Wise, Emma Healy, Rachel Schonberger, Thomas Massion, Liam Lecka, Junie Burns, Marie Mendoza

Overview

I spent two academic quarters working as part of a multidisciplinary team of undergraduate and graduate students to empower high school journalists in the Chicago area.

In our first ten weeks we conducted extensive landscape and user research to understand high school journalism programs. In the second phase of our project we created a comprehensive social media guide for high school journalists, which will be published on the Knight Lab website in the fall of 2020.

User Interviews

First, we interviewed the most knowledgeable experts (and fiercest critics) of high school newsrooms: high school journalism teachers. We learned about the biggest challenges they face, the resources they have and wish they had, the most impactful lessons they've learned from their students, and why they love teaching journalism. Based on the 15 interviews we conducted in the Chicago area, our team created a journalism adviser persona to guide further research and keep our design process grounded in the needs of our user.

Then, we started reaching out to student journalists. I was able to visit two high school journalism classrooms and see firsthand how their newsrooms operate. As a team, we conducted 10 interviews with students to find out their motivations for joining the newspaper and their frustrations with the publishing process. Once we wrapped up these interviews, we created another persona for high school journalists.

Audience Survey

As much as I loved speaking to the high school reporters and their pragmatic instructors, our user research was missing one critical component: the readers. It's true that high school newspapers are primarily learning opportunities for student journalists, but it's so much more fun for the reporters when the newspaper actually reaches the high school community.

For our project, we wanted to know how high schoolers consume news on all levels - national, local, and school. To do this, we surveyed high schoolers in the Chicago area. We asked students about their engagement with news apps and news on social media platforms. We also asked about their social media habits and how much they use their phones. Insights

Following ten weeks of work, we condensed our research into a few key insights that capture the most pressing and compelling challenges facing high school journalism programs.

  1. High school newspapers struggle to reach their audience because their audience gravitates towards social media to get the news.
  2. Newspaper staffs have high turnover, making it difficult for them to maintain quality and implement changes.
  3. Articles lose relevance when they are published in print rather than online, but print attracts more readers than a website.
  4. Advisers teach the students how to be journalists, but can’t teach them how to use new software or leverage social media.
  5. Successful high school journalism programs have a lot of resources and support from their school administration - but not all papers have them.

Designing a Solution

Based on the insights we gathered from our research, our team determined that the biggest opportunity area to bolster high school journalism programs is social media. We decided to compile resources into a social media guide specific for high school journalism programs.

As a team, we decided to cover the following topics:

  1. Branding 101
  2. Instagram Strategy
  3. Social Media Ethics for Journalists
  4. FAQ for Journalism Advisers

I wrote the sections “Social Media Ethics for Journalists” and “FAQ for Journalism Advisers.” I wrote a featured article “Insights: Design Research in Youth Media” with my team member Rachel Schonberger to sum up our research and give context to the social media guide.

We used Notion to create our social media guide. In Fall of 2020, our Notion website will be fed into the Knight Lab website’s CMS system, and our social media guide will be published and shared with high school journalism programs.

The social media guide will provide high school journalists with the resources they need to establish an effective social media presence, and will provide journalism advisers with the resources they need to monitor the accounts, teach ethical online journalism practices, and boost their club or class attendance.
